CHAPTER 1
THE PROBLEM AND ITS SCOPE
Introduction
University student support offices, such as guidance and counseling centers, are recognized as essential components for fostering student success, promoting academic persistence, and addressing personal and emotional challenges. These offices provide critical services including counseling, academic advising, resource facilitation, and empowerment programs. All of these directly contribute to student well-being, retention, and the overall quality of campus life (Mabotha, 2026). Effective management, defined by clear processes, qualified personnel, and appropriate technological tools, remains fundamental to delivering these services efficiently and reliably. In recent years, rising student enrollments and increasingly complex student needs, worsened by the COVID-19 pandemic’s impact on demand for accessible and digital support, have made efficient administrative operations more important than ever (Benavides et al., 2023).
Globally, higher education institutions are undergoing rapid digital transformation to improve student services and administrative effectiveness. Many countries now prioritize digital tools to increase information accessibility, speed up request processing, and reduce paperwork in student support offices (Mabotha, 2026). In the Philippines, this direction receives strong support from national policy. The Commission on Higher Education (CHED) actively promotes digitalization and flexible delivery in state universities and colleges (SUCs) through directives such as CMO No. 4, s. 2020 (Guidelines on the Implementation of Flexible Learning) and CMO No. 8, s. 2021 (Guidelines on the Implementation of Flexible Delivery of Student Affairs and Services Programs During the COVID-19 Pandemic), which encourage administrative modernization, flexible modes, and student-centered services to create more efficient systems (Commission on Higher Education, 2020; 2021). These policies seek to reduce dependence on manual processes, improve data accuracy, and enhance the student experience in public higher education.
Despite these national priorities, many Philippine SUCs, especially satellite campuses such as NORSU-Guihulngan, continue to rely on fragmented, paper-based systems for core student support functions. This creates significant administrative bottlenecks due to inefficient and unreliable record-keeping. A clear example is the university’s CARE Center (Counseling, Assessment, Resources, and Empowerment), which handles a high volume of complex processes. These include coordinating counseling sessions that require departmental approval or referral, managing the full NORSU Admission Test (NAT) cycle (applications, scheduling, and results), processing scholarship applications, conducting student needs assessments (via surveys leading to orientations, symposia, or other events based on popular topics), providing additional support resources (e.g., for working students, indigenous peoples, orphans, and similar groups), and organizing enhancement/empowerment programs with attendance tracking, proof of participation, and feedback/evaluation records.
The Center’s dependence on physical logbooks and filing cabinets for tracking these services, including manual time-in/out logging for student visits and requests for paper-based clearances or other administrative needs, results in serious operational challenges. Managing multi-step NAT applications and scholarship forms requires cumbersome manual handling. Coordinating counseling depends on communication between the CARE Center and academic departments, but this remains inefficient without a shared digital platform. Consolidating data from needs assessments to plan relevant seminars/symposia and tracking attendance for programs are likewise manual and paper-intensive. These tasks consume many hours of staff time each day on data entry, filing, and verification. Digital tools could streamline them significantly while reducing the risk of errors and data loss.
While larger institutions often adopt enterprise-level systems, a noticeable gap remains in affordable, tailored solutions suitable for small campus offices like the CARE Center at NORSU-Guihulngan. These offices need simplicity and a strong focus on core services rather than complex infrastructure (Santos & Reyes, 2025). To address this gap, the present study develops a CARE Center Management System intended to centralize and secure student records, automate routine tracking, and digitize administrative workflows.
The purpose of this study is to analyze current manual processes, identify major inefficiencies in record management and service coordination, design a practical digital system, and evaluate its potential to improve operational reliability. By replacing paper-based logs and scattered files with an integrated digital platform, the research aims to reduce administrative workload, increase data accuracy, and free up staff time for more direct student support. The resulting system offers a scalable model that other small campuses in the Philippine university system could adapt.

Theoretical Framework of the Study
This study is grounded in the Technology Acceptance Model (TAM), a theoretical framework developed by Davis (1989) that explains the determinants of technology adoption. According to TAM, user acceptance of an information system is primarily driven by two core perceptions: Perceived Usefulness (PU) and Perceived Ease of Use (PEOU). These factors shape a user's attitude toward the technology, which influences their behavioral intention to use it and their actual usage behavior.
The TAM framework provides a critical lens for this research, as the success of the proposed CARE Center Management System is contingent upon its acceptance by both staff and students at NORSU-Guihulngan. The model will be used to evaluate whether the system's design effectively addresses the key drivers of adoption.

Figure 1. The Technology Acceptance Model (TAM) as Applied to the CARE Center Management System.
Perceived Usefulness (PU) is defined as the degree to which a user believes that the system will enhance their performance. Within the context of the CARE Center, this translates to tangible benefits across all its services. For example, the ability to instantly retrieve student records accelerates processes like scholarship verification and counseling referrals. Similarly, automated tracking of service requests and student visits replaces inefficient paper logbooks, directly improving the management of NAT applications, event attendance, and daily office logistics. These capabilities collectively lead to higher productivity, faster service delivery, and more reliable data for all counseling, assessment, resource, and enhancement functions.
Perceived Ease of Use (PEOU) refers to the degree to which a user expects the system will be free of effort. For the CARE Center system, this will be achieved through an intuitive interface and streamlined workflows that simplify complex tasks. For students, this means straightforward processes for submitting counseling requests, for office visit in getting paperwork like clearance or certificates given to them, as well as event attendance because of digitizing it rather than mostly paper based procedure. For staff, it entails efficient management of approvals, applicant lists, and student records through clear navigation and minimal clicks. A low learning curve for both user groups is essential, ensuring the system feels like a helpful tool rather than a burdensome complication. 
By applying TAM, this study can systematically assess whether the developed system aligns with user expectations for both utility and usability. A positive perception on these dimensions is anticipated to foster greater willingness to use the system. This outcome would achieve the study's ultimate objective of transforming the CARE Center's service delivery into a more efficient, accessible, and satisfactory experience for all stakeholders.
Conceptual Framework of the Study
This study is guided by the Rapid Application Development (RAD) model as its conceptual framework. The RAD methodology is chosen for its iterative, user-centered approach, which is essential for developing the proposed CARE Center Management System to address the specific challenges and goals defined in this research.

Figure 2. Conceptual Design Of NORSU-GUIHULNGAN CARE Center Management System.
The process begins with Requirements Planning, which is driven by the findings from Statement Of the Problem Question 1 and 2. Data gathered on current procedures, and challenges related to Counseling, Assessment, Resource, and Enhancement services will form the foundational requirements for the system. This is where challenges are written and noted down in order to process what the system will need that will be processed and decide in the next phase.
The next phase is User Design phase which focuses on creating prototypes that embody the key features identified in Statement Of the Problem Question 3. These features are designed to streamline a centralized digital database to consolidate records, an online service portal for requests and applications, a digital check-in system to replace the manual logbook, an automated notification module for reminders, a digital event attendance and management tool for events, and a reporting dashboard for service insights, all designed to digitize the CARE Center's administrative workflows. These features are designed one by one such as their front designs as well as their function flow and design. This is to be sure that it will go smoothly developed in the Construction phase as it is a rapid development phase of the model.
During the Rapid Construction phase, the system is built iteratively. Each development cycle produces a functional prototype that is tested and refined based on continuous feedback, ensuring the final product is robust and user-friendly.
Finally, the Cutover phase involves pilot testing the system to evaluate its performance against the expected outcomes outlined in Statement Of the Problem Question 4. This phase assesses the system's real-world impact on Efficiency, Ease of Use, Accessibility, Timeliness, and User Satisfaction.
Throughout this entire RAD process, continuous engagement with CARE Center staff and students ensures the system remains aligned with user needs. The final output of this framework is a tailored digital management system designed to effectively modernize service delivery at the NORSU CARE Center, as defined by the study's specific aims.
Operational Definitions of Terms
This section defines the key terms used in the study based on their specific function and measurement within the context of developing the CARE Center Management System.
CARE Center Management System: The web-based software developed in this study to digitize the administrative workflows of the NORSU CARE Center. It is operationally defined by its core functions: an online portal for service requests (counseling, NAT, scholarships), a centralized digital record system, automated notifications, and a tracking dashboard, all designed to replace paper-based logbooks and files.
Counseling Services: The process of providing professional guidance to students, operationalized in this study as a service that requires a request (either student-initiated with department approval or staff-referred) and is managed through the system's scheduling and record-keeping features.
Assessment Services: The administrative functions related to student evaluation and data collection. This is operationalized as the management of the NORSU Admission Test (NAT) cycle (applications, scheduling, results), the maintenance of student profiles, and the conduction of needs assessments that inform seminar topics.
Digital Check-In System: A feature that allows students to log their visit to the CARE Center electronically, replacing the manual paper logbook for clearances and general inquiries.
Resource Provision: The facilitation of additional student support. This is operationalized as the processes for applying for specific support categories (e.g., working student, indigent) and for managing scholarship applications through the digital system.
Enhancement Programs: The activities aimed at student development, such as seminars and workshops. This is operationalized through the system's features for event management, digital attendance logging, and feedback collection.
Operational Efficiency: The improvement in the productivity of CARE Center workflows. It is measured by a reduction in the time staff spend on manual tasks like data entry, a decrease in the processing time for services like clearance issuance, and a lower error rate in record-keeping.
Accessibility: The ease with which students can access services remotely. It is measured by the availability of online functions (e.g., submitting a request, checking status) outside of office hours and without a physical visit.
Timeliness: The speed of service delivery. It is measured by comparing the average time to complete a service request (e.g., from submission to resolution) before and after the system's implementation.
User Satisfaction: The perceived effectiveness and ease of use of the services from the perspective of students and staff. It is measured using a Likert-scale survey focusing on the clarity, convenience, and reliability of the new digital process.
Scope and Limitation of the Study
This study will focus on the design and development of a CARE Center Management System specifically for the NORSU CARE Center at the Guihulngan City Campus. The system will cover the core administrative functions of the Center's operations, including: managing requests and appointments for counseling; handling applications, scheduling, and records for the NORSU Admission Test (NAT); processing scholarship applications; and tracking attendance and collecting feedback for enhancement programs. The primary goal is to replace physical logbooks and filing cabinets with a centralized digital platform for record-keeping, thereby addressing the root causes of data fragmentation and administrative bottlenecks.
The system will be developed as a web-based application, accessible to students, CARE Center staff, and authorized administrators. It will include user authentication with role-based access control, a dashboard for managing service requests and student records, and modules for automated notifications, scheduling, and generating basic reports on service utilization.
The development will follow the Rapid Application Development (RAD) model, incorporating feedback from CARE Center staff and a select group of students. The evaluation will be based on a pilot test assessing the system's usability and its perceived impact on daily operational efficiency within the Guihulngan campus.
Limitations: The study acknowledges several limitations. The prototype is designed for the specific context of the Guihulngan CARE Center; its applicability to larger campuses or different administrative units may be limited. Due to thesis-level resource constraints, the system will implement basic security measures and will not include advanced features like psychological test automation, detailed clinical note-taking modules, or full mobile application development. The evaluation's findings, derived from a small user sample over a short period, will indicate preliminary trends rather than provide statistically generalizable results on long-term effectiveness. The system's functionality also assumes basic internet connectivity and user digital literacy and does not automate the core, professional delivery of counseling or empowerment services themselves. These delimitations ensure the project remains focused, feasible, and directly addresses the identified inefficiencies in administrative record-keeping.
Research Methodology
This study employs a Descriptive-Developmental Research design to systematically investigate and address the inefficiencies of the current manual processes at the CARE Center of Negros Oriental State University – Guihulngan City Campus (NORSU-G). The descriptive component systematically collects and analyzes detailed data on existing CARE Center operations, including core services such as counseling coordination (with departmental approvals/referrals), assessment activities (NAT handling, student profiles, needs assessment surveys), resource provision (support forms for working students, indigenous peoples, orphans, scholarships), and enhancement programs (activity implementation, attendance tracking, feedback/evaluation). This phase identifies strengths, weaknesses, bottlenecks, and user perceptions (from staff and students) related to paper-based tools like physical logbooks, manual filing, clearances, and fragmented documentation—highlighting issues such as time delays, data inaccuracies, record loss risks, and administrative workload.
The developmental component builds directly on these findings by designing, developing, and prototyping a web-based CARE Center Management System tailored to resolve identified problems. The system automates and centralizes key workflows—such as digital counseling requests/approvals, NAT application/scheduling/results management, online resource/support forms, survey aggregation for event planning (orientations/symposia), attendance/proof tracking, scholarship processing, and feedback collection—aiming to enhance efficiency, reduce errors, improve data security/accessibility, and allow staff to prioritize direct student support. Development follows an iterative, user-centered process involving requirements gathering (via interviews, observations, and surveys), system design (e.g., use-case diagrams, database schema), prototyping/coding, testing (functionality, usability, security), and initial evaluation for refinement, ensuring the system is practical, scalable, and aligned with the resource-constrained environment of a Philippine SUC satellite campus.
Research Procedure
The research procedure will commence with obtaining the required permissions from the course instructor, the CARE Center coordinator, and the administration of Negros Oriental State University – Guihulngan City Campus (NORSU-G) to conduct the study and access relevant offices, staff, and student respondents. Upon approval, the researchers will proceed with the data-gathering phase, which includes structured interviews with CARE Center staff and distribution of validated questionnaires to selected students and personnel. These instruments will collect comprehensive information on the current manual processes across the four core services: counseling (including referral and approval workflows), assessment (NAT administration, student profiles, needs assessment surveys), resource provision (support forms, scholarship applications), and enhancement programs (activity implementation, attendance tracking, feedback/evaluation), as well as associated administrative tasks such as physical logbook entries, paper clearances, and record-keeping.
Once the questionnaires and interview responses are collected, the data will be carefully organized, analyzed to identify major inefficiencies, bottlenecks, user pain points, and functional/non-functional requirements for the proposed system. Drawing from these findings, the researchers will design and develop the web-based CARE Center Management System, incorporating essential features such as user authentication with role-based access (staff, students, department approvers), digital counseling request and referral modules, NAT application/scheduling/results dashboard, online needs assessment survey tools with automated topic aggregation, resource/support request forms (e.g., for working students, indigenous peoples, orphans), scholarship application processing, enhancement program registration/attendance tracking with proof generation, feedback/evaluation forms, and secure centralized student record management.
The developed prototype will undergo rigorous testing through simulated CARE Center scenarios involving actual users (staff and selected students) to assess functionality, usability, efficiency, accessibility, timeliness, and overall satisfaction. Iterative refinements will be made based on test feedback, ensuring the system effectively addresses the identified challenges while remaining practical and sustainable in the resource-constrained environment of NORSU-Guihulngan.
Research Respondents 
The respondents of this study will be the selected students, CARE Center staff, and faculty members from Negros Oriental State University – Guihulngan City Campus (NORSU-G). These individuals were purposively chosen because they are directly involved in or affected by the CARE Center's operations and represent the primary users of both the current manual processes and the proposed web-based CARE Center Management System.
The student respondents include enrolled students from various colleges and year levels who have previously availed of CARE Center services, such as counseling sessions, needs assessment surveys, resource support requests (e.g., for working students, indigenous peoples, or orphans), scholarship applications, enhancement program participation, or administrative requests (e.g., clearances or logbook entries). Their inclusion ensures diverse perspectives on accessibility, timeliness, ease of use, and overall satisfaction with the existing and proposed systems.
The CARE Center staff respondents consist of the guidance counselors, assessment coordinators, administrative personnel, and other office personnel responsible for delivering and managing the core services (counseling coordination, assessment activities including NAT, resource provision, and enhancement programs). They provide critical insights into operational workflows, daily challenges, documentation burdens, and functional requirements for system improvement.
Additionally, selected faculty members (particularly those from academic departments involved in counseling referrals or approvals) will serve as respondents to capture inter-departmental coordination perspectives and feedback on how the system can better support student referrals and holistic development processes.
This combination of respondents—students as end-users, CARE staff as primary operators, and faculty as key collaborators—ensures a comprehensive assessment of the current manual system's inefficiencies and the proposed digital solution's potential to enhance service delivery, data management, and student support in the resource-constrained environment of NORSU-Guihulngan.
Research Environment 
The research environment of this study will be at Negros Oriental State University – Guihulngan City Campus (NORSU-G). As the primary setting where the current manual processes of the CARE Center (Counseling, Assessment, Resources, and Enhancement/Empowerment) are implemented, NORSU-G provides a relevant and practical context for both the investigation of existing inefficiencies and the development and application of the proposed web-based CARE Center Management System.
Negros Oriental State University – Guihulngan City Campus (NORSU-G) is located along the National Highway in Barangay Poblacion, Guihulngan City, Negros Oriental, Philippines, in front of Governor William Villegas Memorial Hospital. As a satellite campus of NORSU, it serves a diverse student population from rural and nearby communities, delivering essential student support services including counseling coordination, admission and needs assessment (NAT), resource provision for vulnerable groups, scholarship processing, and enhancement programs. The campus's resource-constrained environment, reliance on paper-based tools (e.g., physical logbooks, manual forms, and filing cabinets), and need for efficient, accessible student services make it an ideal and representative setting for this descriptive-developmental study aimed at modernizing administrative workflows in a Philippine state university and college (SUC) satellite context
