INTRODUCTION
Healthcare management within academic institutions serves as a critical support system for student well-being, academic performance, and overall campus safety. An efficient clinic operation not only addresses immediate medical needs but also maintains comprehensive health records that inform long-term student care and institutional health policies. In higher education settings such as Negros Oriental State University – Guihulngan Campus, the clinic provides essential services including consultations, medication dispensing, medical certifications, and emergency response, forming a foundational component of campus welfare infrastructure. Campus clinics play a dual role—providing student care and serving as practical health management environments (Perini, 2023). Their efficiency directly affects student well-being and institutional resilience.
Globally, the push for digital health transformation has been strong. The World Health Organization urges member states to adopt eHealth strategies for equitable, affordable care (WHO, 2005), reinforced by the Global Strategy on Digital Health 2020–2027, which emphasizes interoperability and user-centered design (WHO, 2020).
In the Philippines, the Universal Health Care Act (RA 11223) mandates digital integration in healthcare, with DOH initiatives promoting electronic records and streamlined systems. These policies aim to improve data accuracy, reduce administrative burdens, and enhance patient safety (Perini, 2023). However, implementation often lags in resource-limited settings like provincial campus clinics. While national frameworks provide vision, many small institutions still rely on paper-based systems due to budget constraints, technical gaps, and misaligned solutions.
This gap is evident at Negros Oriental State University – Guihulngan Campus, where clinic operations remain manual despite digital advancements elsewhere. The transition here requires more than policy compliance—it needs context-sensitive, affordable, and user-friendly innovation tailored to frontline realities. This thesis addresses that need by developing a practical digital clinic system that aligns with global and national digital health goals while meeting the specific constraints and workflows of a small campus clinic.
While digital health frameworks advance globally and nationally, many small-scale health facilities—including campus clinics in provincial universities—continue to operate through inefficient, paper-reliant systems (DOH, 2020). This gap is evident at Negros Oriental State University – Guihulngan Campus, where clinic services remain largely manual despite a prior attempt at digitization via the now-defunct “NORSU-GUIHULNGAN CLINIC E-RECORD SYSTEM.” After computers were reformatted, only the system’s dashboard remains accessible, rendering its functional modules unusable and forcing staff back to handwritten logs and forms.
Interviews with clinic personnel confirm persistent inefficiencies, including redundant data entry across multiple medical documents—a practice known to increase error rates and administrative time in healthcare settings (Chen et al., 2021). Staff also report recurring discrepancies in medicine inventory records, a common issue in manual stock management that disrupts supply continuity and patient care (Lorenzo et al., 2015). Without a centralized digital record system, patient histories remain fragmented across paper logs, complicating follow-up care and health reporting—a challenge noted in similar small-clinic contexts in Philippine rural health units (Santos, 2022).
This operational reality underscores a critical implementation gap: while policies such as the Universal Health Care Act (RA 11223) promote integrated digital health systems, frontline clinics like that of NORSU Guihulngan lack sustainable, context-appropriate technological support. The failure of the earlier student-built system further highlights the need for resilient, user-informed design in health information technology—an area where participatory development has shown improved adoption and effectiveness in resource-constrained settings (Mamlin et al., 2006).
The clinic at NORSU Guihulngan Campus continues to rely on inefficient manual systems and a nonfunctional digital platform, undermining service quality, operational efficiency, and alignment with national and global digitalhealth directives. Key issues include redundant and errorprone documentation, where staff must repeatedly handwrite the same patient information across multiple forms. Compounding this, medicine inventory is tracked manually, resulting in frequent stock discrepancies that disrupt patient care and supply continuity, a common challenge in resourcelimited health settings (Lorenzo et al., 2015).
Further complicating clinic operations is the absence of a centralized, accessible patientrecord system. Student health data remain fragmented across paper logs and unlinked forms, hindering continuity of care, trend analysis, and timely reporting—a gap also observed in other small Philippine health facilities (Santos & Cruz, 2022). The failure of the earlier “NORSUGUIHULNGAN CLINIC ERECORD SYSTEM” highlights a broader sustainability issue: without usercentered design and resilient architecture, locally developed healthIT solutions often become obsolete, reinforcing reliance on paperbased workflows (Mamlin et al., 2006).
These operational shortcomings stand in direct contrast to the digitalhealth integration goals of the Universal Health Care Act (RA 11223) and the WHO’s emphasis on interoperable, equitable health information systems. Consequently, there is a pressing need for a sustainable, webbased clinic management system that streamlines documentation, ensures accurate inventory tracking, centralizes patient records, and is designed for longterm usability in a smallcampus context.
To address these identified challenges, this study proposes the development and implementation of “NORSU-GUIHULNGAN CLINIC E-RECORD SYSTEM VERSION 2.0”—a redesigned, web-based clinic management system built with sustainability, usability, and local adaptability at its core. Leveraging modern web technologies and responsive interface design, the system will implement a structured digital workflow centers around a master patient record that automatically populates all subsequent medical documents.
At the heart of the system is a master Patient Record Form that serves as a single source of truth—a design principle aligned with successful template-based health information systems such as OpenMRS, which has demonstrated improved data consistency and reduced redundant entry in resource-conscious settings (Mamlin et al., 2006). From this centralized record, the system will auto-populate standardized, editable templates for prescriptions, excuse slips, referral forms, and medical certificates, significantly cutting documentation time while preserving clinician flexibility. Each form will feature built-in PDF export functionality, enabling instant printing and digital archiving without manual reformatting.
The system will incorporate a real-time medicine inventory module with automated stock tracking, low-quantity alerts, and monthly consumption reporting, addressing the inventory discrepancies commonly reported in manual clinic systems (Lorenzo et al., 2015). Role-based access will ensure data security and appropriate workflow separation between students, clinic staff, and administrators. By adopting an iterative, user-centered development approach informed directly by clinic personnel, the system aims to avoid the usability and sustainability pitfalls that rendered the previous version obsolete, thereby supporting the digital health integration goals outlined in the Philippine Universal Health Care Act (RA 11223) and contributing to the WHO’s vision of equitable, efficient health service delivery.
This study aims to design, develop, and implement a sustainable, web-based clinic management system that addresses the operational inefficiencies identified at the NORSU Guihulngan Campus clinic. The general objective is to create a fully functional digital platform—titled “NORSU-GUIHULNGAN CLINIC E-RECORD SYSTEM VERSION 2.0”—that centralizes patient records, automates the generation of key medical forms, and provides accurate, real-time medicine inventory tracking.
By fulfilling these objectives, the study seeks to deliver a practical, locally adapted digital tool that not only resolves the documentation and inventory challenges faced by the clinic but also aligns with broader digital health initiatives under the Philippine Universal Health Care Act, thereby contributing a scalable model for small-campus clinic digitization in resource-conscious settings.


Statement of the Problem
The clinic of Negros Oriental State University – Guihulngan Campus currently relies on predominantly manual and semi-digital processes for managing patient records, medical forms, and medicine inventory. These practices often result in delayed transactions, difficulty in retrieving records, data inconsistencies, and challenges in monitoring medicine availability. Such limitations affect the efficiency of clinic operations and the quality of health services provided to students and personnel.
This study aims to develop NORSU-Guihulngan Clinic E-Record System Version 2.0, an enhanced web-based clinic management system designed to streamline clinic workflows through automated form generation, centralized patient records, and improved medicine inventory management.
Specifically, the study seeks to answer the following research questions:
1. What are the current processes and workflows of the NORSU-Guihulngan Campus clinic in terms of:
1.1 Patient record management and documentation?
1.2 Medicine inventory tracking and dispensation?
1.3 Medical form generation and processing?
1.4 Reporting and data management?

2. What problems are encountered by clinic staff and students in using the current system in terms of:
2.1 Time efficiency and workflow delays?
2.2 Data accuracy and error frequency?
2.3 Record accessibility and retrieval?
2.4 Resource management and inventory control?

3. What essential features and components are required in the proposed NORSU-Guihulngan Clinic E-Record System Version 2.0 in terms of:
3.1 Patient record management and automated medical forms?
3.2 Medicine inventory tracking and reporting?
3.3 User access control and data security?
3.4 System usability and interface design?

4. How effective is the developed web-based clinic management system in addressing the identified problems in terms of:
4.1 Reducing administrative time and manual workload?
4.2 Improving data accuracy and consistency?
4.3 Enhancing service delivery and user satisfaction?

Review of Related Literature
This review examines existing studies and technological developments related to web-based healthcare systems, electronic medical records, clinic management platforms, and digital form automation, with particular focus on implementations in educational and resource-limited settings.
Web-based Clinic Management System (CMS) by Muhammad and Garba (2019) presents a directly comparable implementation of a clinic management system at Sule Lamido University in Nigeria—an academic institution facing similar challenges to NORSU Guihulngan Campus. This research is particularly valuable because it addresses almost identical operational contexts: a university clinic transitioning from manual to digital systems, serving student populations, and managing constrained resources. The authors identify parallel problems including "lack of immediate record storage and retrieval," "paper-based approach," "error-prone manual calculations," and difficulties in exchanging patient information between healthcare practitioners—issues that mirror those documented in the NORSU clinic during our interviews.

Muhammad and Garba's (2019) system design offers practical implementation insights for the NORSU clinic system. Their CMS includes patient appointment scheduling, medical report generation, online consultation features, inventory management, and staff record management—functionalities that align closely with the current study's objectives. Their use of the System Development Life Cycle (SDLC) methodology and web-based implementation using similar technologies (though unspecified, but presumably PHP/MySQL given the publication context) provides a validated roadmap for developing the NORSU clinic system. The researchers' successful implementation demonstrates that "the system complies with the specified requirements" and achieves its objectives of minimizing costs, reducing difficulties, and enhancing clinic service effectiveness—outcomes directly relevant to the NORSU clinic system.

Understanding Adoption of Electronic Medical Records (EMRs) during a Health Emergency: An Analysis of EMR Usage Logs from Rural Health Facilities in the Philippines by Acacio-Claro et al. (2023) provides critical insights into the implementation challenges and adoption patterns of electronic medical records in resource-constrained Philippine settings. Analyzing over 270,000 EMR usage logs from six rural health units in Western Visayas, the researchers found that EMR usage during the COVID-19 pandemic ranged from less than one hour to more than eight hours daily, with statistically significant variations in feature utilization between pre-pandemic and pandemic periods. This research is particularly relevant to the current study as it demonstrates that "progressive use from basic to complete [EMR features] may vary among users" in rural healthcare environments similar to campus clinic settings.

The study highlights several key findings pertinent to developing a clinic management system for NORSU Guihulngan Campus. First, it reveals that basic EMR features were more frequently utilized than advanced functionalities, suggesting that initial system implementations should prioritize core functionalities before expanding to complex modules. Second, the research documents how public health emergencies can significantly impact technology adoption patterns, underscoring the importance of designing systems that remain functional during operational disruptions. Third, the authors note that "complete adoption of EMR features" was achieved by some users despite infrequent use of advanced capabilities, indicating that comprehensive system design does not guarantee consistent utilization of all features.

OpenMRS as a global good: Impact, opportunities, challenges, and lessons learned from fifteen years of implementation by Verma et al. (2021) documents the successful deployment of OpenMRS across over 62 countries, serving millions of patients. The research identifies consistent benefits including streamlined healthcare delivery, improved reporting, enhanced data quality for decision-making, and better interoperability—all objectives directly relevant to developing a clinic management system for NORSU Guihulngan Campus. However, the study also reveals critical implementation challenges that are particularly instructive for resource-constrained environments: difficulty finding skilled technical staff, resistance from clinical personnel during digital transitions, inadequate training programs, infrastructure limitations, and missing specific functionalities in generic systems. These findings directly inform the current study's approach of developing a customized, user-centered system with comprehensive training and realistic infrastructure requirements rather than adopting an off-the-shelf solution that may not address the campus clinic's specific operational needs.

The financial perspective presented by Verma et al. (2021)—noting that 4–8 million USD has been invested in OpenMRS development—underscores the substantial resources required for sustainable health information systems. Their conclusion that "continued investment... is needed to sustain and scale its impact" emphasizes the importance of planning beyond initial implementation to include long-term maintenance, updates, and local capacity building. For the NORSU clinic system, this translates into designing for sustainability from the outset, with clear documentation, maintainable code structure, and institutional support mechanisms to prevent the system obsolescence that affected the previous clinic E-record system. The study's evidence of positive returns on investment through improved healthcare quality and efficiency provides strong justification for developing a tailored digital solution for the campus clinic despite resource constraints.

Fast, Structured Clinical Documentation via Contextual Autocomplete by Gopinath et al. (2020) introduces a revolutionary approach to clinical documentation through machine learning-based autocompletion systems. Their research demonstrates that contextual autocomplete mechanisms can reduce keystroke burden for clinical concepts by 67% in live hospital settings, addressing a critical challenge in healthcare documentation where clinicians spend excessive time on electronic health records rather than patient care. The system leverages shallow neural networks to suggest relevant clinical concepts in real-time as doctors draft notes, simultaneously structuring documentation with standardized medical vocabulary labels. This approach bridges the gap between clinicians' preference for natural language documentation and the need for structured, machine-readable data—a challenge equally relevant to the NORSU clinic context where staff must balance detailed documentation with limited time.

While the advanced machine learning approach described may exceed the current scope of the NORSU clinic system, the study's core principle of reducing documentation burden through intelligent assistance is highly applicable. The researchers' observation that "clinical documentation is often noisy, ambiguous, and incomplete" when created under time pressure directly mirrors the challenges observed in manual campus clinic documentation. Their solution, which automatically structures notes as they're written and reduces redundant information entry—offers valuable design insights for developing form automation features in simpler systems. The concept of "live-tagging" clinical concepts and automatically populating related sections of documentation aligns closely with the current study's objective of creating a master patient record that auto-populates multiple medical forms, demonstrating that even basic automation can deliver substantial efficiency gains comparable to those achieved through more sophisticated AI approaches.

Optimizing Digital Transition: Addressing Challenges in Modernizing Inventory Systems in Primary Healthcare Facilities by Bolaños et al. (2024) provides critical insights into the specific challenges of implementing digital inventory systems in Philippine healthcare settings—findings that are directly applicable to the medicine inventory module of the NORSU clinic management system. This Philippine-based research, conducted in the National Capital Region, identifies three primary barriers to successful digital transition: lack of infrastructure, inadequate training, and fractured supply chains. These challenges mirror potential implementation hurdles at NORSU Guihulngan Campus, where similar resource constraints and training limitations may affect the adoption of digital inventory systems. The study's use of quantitative analysis (ANOVA to establish relationships between user satisfaction and operational effectiveness) and qualitative thematic analysis provides a methodological framework that could inform the evaluation phase of the current research.

Notably, Bolaños et al. (2024) found that despite these challenges, digital transformation "has the potential to enhance inventory management and healthcare delivery," validating the current study's focus on developing an automated medicine tracking system. Their emphasis on focused training and robust digital solutions as essential components for optimizing resource management directly supports the NORSU project's approach of combining system development with comprehensive user training. The researchers' conclusion that "significant barriers must be addressed to achieve full adoption" serves as a crucial reminder that technical development alone is insufficient—successful implementation requires addressing infrastructure limitations, providing adequate training, and considering supply chain integration, particularly important for provincial campuses where access to consistent medicine supplies may be challenging.
Theoretical Framework of the Study
This study is anchored on the Technology Acceptance Model (TAM) developed by Davis (1989), which explains how users come to accept and use a technology. TAM posits that an individual’s acceptance of an information system is primarily influenced by two key factors: Perceived Usefulness and Perceived Ease of Use. These factors directly affect users’ attitudes toward the system and their intention to use it.

In the context of the NORSU-Guihulngan Clinic E-Record System Version 2.0, TAM provides a suitable theoretical foundation for evaluating how clinic staff and users perceive and accept the proposed system.

Perceived Usefulness (PU) refers to the degree to which clinic staff believe that using the system will enhance their job performance. In this study, it includes improvements such as faster patient record retrieval, automated form generation, accurate reporting, and efficient medicine inventory management.

Perceived Ease of Use (PEOU) refers to the degree to which users believe that the system will be free of effort. This involves the system’s intuitive interface, clear navigation, minimal learning curve, and ease of performing daily clinic tasks.

According to TAM, when users perceive the system as useful and easy to use, they are more likely to develop a positive attitude toward it, leading to higher acceptance and actual system usage. Applying TAM allows this study to assess whether the developed clinic e-record system meets user expectations and supports effective adoption within the university clinic environment.

Conceptual Framework of the Study
The conceptual framework of the study is guided by the Agile Development Methodology, which emphasizes flexibility, continuous improvement, and active stakeholder involvement throughout the system development life cycle. This methodology is well-suited for the dynamic operational needs of a university clinic.
The Agile framework follows an iterative cycle consisting of Planning, Designing, Developing, Testing, Deploying, and Reviewing.
Planning Phase involves identifying clinic requirements through consultations with clinic staff and administrators. Key functionalities such as patient record management, automated form generation, and medicine inventory tracking are prioritized.
Designing Phase focuses on creating a user-friendly system interface and database structure that supports efficient record management and secure data handling. User feedback is considered to ensure accessibility for both technical and non-technical users.
Development Phase includes the implementation of core system features such as digital patient records, automated medical forms, inventory monitoring, and reporting modules. Features are developed incrementally per Agile sprint.
Testing Phase ensures the system’s functionality, usability, accuracy, and security. Clinic staff are involved in testing to validate real-world usability and system reliability.
Deployment Phase makes the system available for actual clinic operations.
Review and Improvement Phase collects user feedback to identify enhancements and necessary updates. Continuous improvements are deployed to keep the system responsive to evolving clinic needs.





Through Agile methodology, the proposed system becomes an adaptive solution that continuously improves clinic efficiency and service delivery.
Scope and Limitations of the Study
Scope:  
This study will focus on the design and development of a web-based clinic management system specifically for the campus clinic at Negros Oriental State University – Guihulngan Campus (NORSU-G). The system will cover the core functions of clinic operations, including: patient registration and record management, medicine inventory tracking, automated medical form generation, and reporting functionalities.

The system will be developed as a web-based application, accessible to registered students, clinic staff, and authorized administrators. It will include user authentication with role-based access control, a dashboard for managing patient records and medicine inventory, and automated form generation for prescriptions, excuse slips, referral forms, and medical certificates with PDF export capability.

Limitations:
The study will be limited to the NORSU Guihulngan Campus clinic and does not extend to other NORSU campuses or external healthcare facilities. It focuses solely on basic clinic operations and is not designed for comprehensive hospital management systems or specialized medical modules such as laboratory information systems or telemedicine. Additional limitations include the requirement for stable internet or intranet access for system functionality; the exclusion of integration with external systems such as the Philippine Health Insurance Corporation (PhilHealth) or national health databases; and the implementation of basic data security features, with advanced cybersecurity measures such as penetration testing or blockchain-based record keeping falling outside the study's scope.

Operational Definition of Terms
A. SYSTEM & TECHNICAL COMPONENTS
Auto Generated Forms. Medical documents (prescription, excuse slip, referral form, medical certificate) that are automatically populated with patient data from the Master Patient Record Form, while allowing clinic staff to edit additional fields specific to each document type within the system interface.
Digital Form Automation. The system process where patient information entered into the Master Patient Record Form is automatically transferred to corresponding fields in subsequent medical forms, eliminating the need for manual rewriting of identical data across multiple documents.
Master Patient Record Form. The primary data entry interface in the system where all patient information (demographic details, medical history, vital signs) is initially recorded, serving as the single source of truth that supplies data to all other medical documents generated by the system.
NORSU-GUIHULNGAN CLINIC E-RECORD SYSTEM VERSION 2.O. The web-based clinic management system developed in this study, built using PHP, MySQL, and Bootstrap, specifically designed to digitize, streamline, and automate the patient record keeping, medical form generation, and medicine inventory processes at the NORSU Guihulngan Campus clinic.
Web-Based System. A software application accessible through standard web browsers via the campus network, developed using PHP for server-side processing, MySQL for database operations, and Bootstrap for responsive interface design, requiring no local installation on clinic computers.
B. CLINIC PROCESSES & MANAGEMENT
Medicine Inventory Module. The system component that maintains real-time tracking of medicine stock levels, automatically deducts quantities when medicines are dispensed, generates visual alerts when stock falls below predetermined thresholds, and produces monthly consumption reports for procurement planning.
Patient Health History. The chronological digital compilation stored in the system database containing all clinic visits, diagnoses, treatments, prescribed medications, and generated medical forms for an individual student, accessible through authorized user queries.
C. USER ROLES
Clinic Staff. Authorized medical personnel (nurse, doctor) employed at the NORSU Guihulngan Campus clinic who utilize the system to document patient consultations, prescribe medications, generate medical forms, manage inventory, and access patient health records.
Student/Patient. Currently enrolled students at NORSU Guihulngan Campus who are registered in the system, can access their personal medical history through a dedicated portal, and for whom medical records and forms are created during clinic visits.
D. SYSTEM EVALUATION
System Usability. The degree to which clinic staff can effectively, efficiently, and satisfactorily use the system to complete their daily tasks, measured through task completion time, frequency of errors, and subjective satisfaction ratings collected during testing phases.
User Acceptance Testing. The final evaluation phase where actual clinic staff members use the deployed system to perform realistic clinic tasks, providing feedback on functionality, interface design, workflow compatibility, and overall system effectiveness in their operational environment.


RESEARCH METHODOLOGY
Research Method
This study employs a Descriptive-Developmental Research design to comprehensively address the challenges posed by the current manual clinic management system at Negros Oriental State University – Guihulngan Campus (NORSU-G). The descriptive component of the research involves systematically gathering and analyzing detailed information regarding the existing clinic processes. This includes examining core functions such as patient registration, medical record documentation, medicine inventory management, form generation, and reporting procedures. By assessing these areas, the study aims to identify the strengths and weaknesses inherent in the current system, as well as to understand the difficulties and perceptions experienced by both clinic staff and student patients. This phase helps to establish a clear picture of the existing situation, highlighting specific issues such as inefficiencies, documentation errors, inventory discrepancies, and user dissatisfaction.

The developmental component follows, focusing on the design, creation, and implementation of a web-based clinic management system that directly responds to the problems uncovered in the descriptive phase. This system is built to enhance and automate the critical functions of clinic operations, aiming to streamline documentation, reduce human error, improve inventory accuracy, and facilitate timely medical service delivery. The development process involves gathering functional and non-functional requirements, system design, coding, testing, and deployment tailored to the needs of NORSU-G clinic.
Research Procedure
The research procedure will begin with securing the necessary permissions from the course instructor and the administration of Negros Oriental State University – Guihulngan Campus (NORSU-G) to conduct the study. Once approval is granted, the researchers will proceed with the data-gathering phase, which involves conducting interviews and distributing questionnaires to selected respondents, including clinic staff and students, to collect detailed information about the current manual clinic system. These instruments aim to understand the system's core functions, challenges, and user perceptions.

After collecting the completed questionnaires, the data will undergo careful analysis and interpretation to identify key issues and determine the requirements for developing the proposed web-based clinic management system. Using these insights, the researchers will design and develop the system with essential features such as patient registration, electronic health records, medicine inventory tracking, automated form generation (prescriptions, excuse slips, referral forms, medical certificates), and reporting modules. The system will then be tested through simulated clinic scenarios involving actual users to evaluate its performance and usability.
Research Respondents
The respondents of this study will be the selected students and clinic staff from Negros Oriental State University – Guihulngan Campus (NORSU-G). These individuals were chosen because they are directly involved in the university's clinic operations and are the primary users of both the current manual system and the proposed web-based clinic management system. The student respondents include patients from various colleges and year levels who have previously utilized clinic services, while the clinic staff include the school nurse, consulting physician, and administrative personnel who manage and oversee the healthcare delivery process.
Research Environment
The research environment of this study will be at Negros Oriental State University – Guihulngan Campus (NORSU-G). As the primary setting where the current manual clinic management system is implemented, NORSU-G provides a relevant and practical context for both the investigation and application of the proposed web-based clinic management system. Negros Oriental State University – Guihulngan Campus (NORSU-G) is located along the National Highway in Barangay Poblacion, Guihulngan City, Negros Oriental, Philippines in front of Governor William Villegas Memorial Hospital.
