CHAPTER 1
THE PROBLEM AND ITS SCOPE


Introduction
University student support offices, such as guidance and counseling centers, are recognized as essential components for fostering student success, promoting academic persistence, and addressing personal and emotional challenges. These offices provide critical services including counseling, academic advising, resource facilitation, and empowerment programs. All of these directly contribute to student well-being, retention, and the overall quality of campus life (Mabotha, 2026). Effective management, defined by clear processes, qualified personnel, and appropriate technological tools, remains fundamental to delivering these services efficiently and reliably. In recent years, rising student enrollments and increasingly complex student needs, worsened by the COVID-19 pandemic’s impact on demand for accessible and digital support, have made efficient administrative operations more important than ever (Benavides et al., 2023).
Globally, higher education institutions are undergoing rapid digital transformation to improve student services and administrative effectiveness. Many countries now prioritize digital tools to increase information accessibility, speed up request processing, and reduce paperwork in student support offices (Mabotha, 2026). In the Philippines, this direction receives strong support from national policy. The Commission on Higher Education (CHED) actively promotes digitalization and flexible delivery in state universities and colleges (SUCs) through directives such as CMO No. 4, s. 2020 (Guidelines on the Implementation of Flexible Learning) and CMO No. 8, s. 2021 (Guidelines on the Implementation of Flexible Delivery of Student Affairs and Services Programs During the COVID-19 Pandemic), which encourage administrative modernization, flexible modes, and student-centered services to create more efficient systems (Commission on Higher Education, 2020; 2021). These policies seek to reduce dependence on manual processes, improve data accuracy, and enhance the student experience in public higher education.
Despite these national priorities, many Philippine SUCs, especially satellite campuses such as NORSU-Guihulngan, continue to rely on fragmented, paper-based systems for core student support functions. This situation creates significant administrative bottlenecks due to inefficient and unreliable record-keeping. A clear example is the university’s CARE Center (Counseling, Assessment, Resources, and Empowerment), which handles a high volume of complex processes. These include coordinating counseling sessions that require departmental approval or referral, managing the full NORSU Admission Test (NAT) cycle (applications, scheduling, and results), processing scholarship applications, conducting student needs assessments (via surveys that inform orientations, symposia, and other programs based on identified concerns), providing additional support resources (e.g., for working students, indigenous peoples, orphans, and similar groups), and organizing enhancement or empowerment programs with attendance tracking, proof of participation, and feedback or evaluation records.
The Center’s dependence on physical logbooks and filing cabinets for tracking these services, including manual time-in and time-out logging for student visits and paper-based clearance requests or other administrative transactions, results in serious operational challenges. Managing multi-step NAT applications and scholarship forms requires cumbersome manual handling. Counseling coordination depends on communication between the CARE Center and academic departments, yet this process remains inefficient without a shared digital platform. Likewise, consolidating data from needs assessments to plan relevant seminars or symposia and monitoring attendance during programs remain manual and paper-intensive tasks. These responsibilities consume substantial staff time for data entry, filing, and verification and increase the risk of delays, errors, and data loss.
While larger institutions often adopt enterprise-level systems, a noticeable gap remains in affordable, tailored solutions suitable for small campus offices like the CARE Center at NORSU-Guihulngan. These offices require systems that emphasize simplicity and core service management rather than complex infrastructure (Santos & Reyes, 2025). To address this gap, the present study proposes the development of a CARE Center Management System intended to centralize and secure student records, automate routine tracking, and digitize administrative workflows within the Center.
The purpose of this study is to analyze existing manual processes, identify inefficiencies in record management and service coordination, and design a practical digital system tailored to the operational context of NORSU-Guihulngan. By introducing an integrated digital platform in place of paper-based logs and fragmented documentation, the proposed system aims to support improved administrative organization, enhance data accuracy, and allow CARE personnel to focus more on direct student support. Furthermore, the system is intended to provide a scalable framework that may be adapted by other small campuses within the Philippine university system facing similar administrative challenges.

Theoretical Framework of the Study
This study is grounded in the Technology Acceptance Model (TAM), a theoretical framework developed by Davis (1989) that explains the determinants of technology adoption. According to TAM, user acceptance of an information system is primarily driven by two core perceptions: Perceived Usefulness (PU) and Perceived Ease of Use (PEOU). These factors shape a user's attitude toward the technology, which influences their behavioral intention to use it and their actual usage behavior.
The TAM framework provides a critical lens for this research, as the success of the proposed CARE Center Management System is contingent upon its acceptance by both staff and students at NORSU-Guihulngan. The model will be used to evaluate whether the system's design effectively addresses the key drivers of adoption.

Figure 1. The Technology Acceptance Model (TAM) as Applied to the CARE Center Management System.
Perceived Usefulness (PU) is defined as the degree to which a user believes that the system will enhance their performance. Within the context of the CARE Center, this translates to tangible benefits across all its services. For example, the ability to instantly retrieve student records accelerates processes like scholarship verification and counseling referrals. Similarly, automated tracking of service requests and student visits replaces inefficient paper logbooks, directly improving the management of NAT applications, event attendance, and daily office logistics. These capabilities collectively lead to higher productivity, faster service delivery, and more reliable data for all counseling, assessment, resource, and enhancement functions.
Perceived Ease of Use (PEOU) refers to the degree to which a user expects the system will be free of effort. For the CARE Center system, this will be achieved through an intuitive interface and streamlined workflows that simplify complex tasks. For students, this means straightforward processes for submitting counseling requests, for office visit in getting paperwork like clearance or certificates given to them, as well as event attendance because of digitizing it rather than mostly paper based procedure. For staff, it entails efficient management of approvals, applicant lists, and student records through clear navigation and minimal clicks. A low learning curve for both user groups is essential, ensuring the system feels like a helpful tool rather than a burdensome complication. 
By applying TAM, this study can systematically assess whether the developed system aligns with user expectations for both utility and usability. A positive perception on these dimensions is anticipated to foster greater willingness to use the system. This outcome would achieve the study's ultimate objective of transforming the CARE Center's service delivery into a more efficient, accessible, and satisfactory experience for all stakeholders.
Conceptual Framework of the Study
This study is guided by the Rapid Application Development (RAD) model as its conceptual framework. The RAD methodology is chosen for its iterative, user-centered approach, which is essential for developing the proposed CARE Center Management System to address the specific challenges and goals defined in this research.

Figure 2. Conceptual Design Of NORSU-GUIHULNGAN CARE Center Management System.
The process begins with Requirements Planning, which is driven by the findings from Statement Of the Problem Question 1 and 2. Data gathered on current procedures, and challenges related to Counseling, Assessment, Resource, and Enhancement services will form the foundational requirements for the system. This is where challenges are written and noted down in order to process what the system will need that will be processed and decide in the next phase.
The next phase is User Design phase which focuses on creating prototypes that embody the key features identified in Statement Of the Problem Question 3. These features are designed to streamline a centralized digital database to consolidate records, an online service portal for requests and applications, a digital check-in system to replace the manual logbook, an automated notification module for reminders, a digital event attendance and management tool for events, and a reporting dashboard for service insights, all designed to digitize the CARE Center's administrative workflows. These features are designed one by one such as their front designs as well as their function flow and design. This is to be sure that it will go smoothly developed in the Construction phase as it is a rapid development phase of the model.
During the Rapid Construction phase, the system is built iteratively. Each development cycle produces a functional prototype that is tested and refined based on continuous feedback, ensuring the final product is robust and user-friendly.
Finally, the Cutover phase involves pilot testing the system to evaluate its performance against the expected outcomes outlined in Statement Of the Problem Question 4. This phase assesses the system's real-world impact on Efficiency, Ease of Use, Accessibility, Timeliness, and User Satisfaction.
Throughout this entire RAD process, continuous engagement with CARE Center staff and students ensures the system remains aligned with user needs. The final output of this framework is a tailored digital management system designed to effectively modernize service delivery at the NORSU CARE Center, as defined by the study's specific aims.
Operational Definitions of Terms
This section defines the key terms used in the study based on their specific function and measurement within the context of developing the CARE Center Management System.
CARE Center Management System: The web-based software developed in this study to digitize the administrative workflows of the NORSU CARE Center. It is operationally defined by its core functions: an online portal for service requests (counseling, NAT, scholarships), a centralized digital record system, automated notifications, and a tracking dashboard, all designed to replace paper-based logbooks and files.
Counseling Services: The process of providing professional guidance to students, operationalized in this study as a service that requires a request (either student-initiated with department approval or staff-referred) and is managed through the system's scheduling and record-keeping features.
Assessment Services: The administrative functions related to student evaluation and data collection. This is operationalized as the management of the NORSU Admission Test (NAT) cycle (applications, scheduling, results), the maintenance of student profiles, and the conduction of needs assessments that inform seminar topics.
Digital Check-In System: A feature that allows students to log their visit to the CARE Center electronically, replacing the manual paper logbook for clearances and general inquiries.
Resource Provision: The facilitation of additional student support. This is operationalized as the processes for applying for specific support categories (e.g., working student, indigent) and for managing scholarship applications through the digital system.
Enhancement Programs: The activities aimed at student development, such as seminars and workshops. This is operationalized through the system's features for event management, digital attendance logging, and feedback collection.
Operational Efficiency: The improvement in the productivity of CARE Center workflows. It is measured by a reduction in the time staff spend on manual tasks like data entry, a decrease in the processing time for services like clearance issuance, and a lower error rate in record-keeping.
Accessibility: The ease with which students can access services remotely. It is measured by the availability of online functions (e.g., submitting a request, checking status) outside of office hours and without a physical visit.
Timeliness: The speed of service delivery. It is measured by comparing the average time to complete a service request (e.g., from submission to resolution) before and after the system's implementation.
User Satisfaction: The perceived effectiveness and ease of use of the services from the perspective of students and staff. It is measured using a Likert-scale survey focusing on the clarity, convenience, and reliability of the new digital process.
Scope and Limitation of the Study
This study will focus on the design and development of a CARE Center Management System specifically for the NORSU CARE Center at the Guihulngan City Campus. The system will cover the core administrative functions of the Center's operations, including: managing requests and appointments for counseling; handling applications, scheduling, and records for the NORSU Admission Test (NAT); processing scholarship applications; and tracking attendance and collecting feedback for enhancement programs. The primary goal is to replace physical logbooks and filing cabinets with a centralized digital platform for record-keeping, thereby addressing the root causes of data fragmentation and administrative bottlenecks.
The system will be developed as a web-based application, accessible to students, CARE Center staff, and authorized administrators. It will include user authentication with role-based access control, a dashboard for managing service requests and student records, and modules for automated notifications, scheduling, and generating basic reports on service utilization.
The development will follow the Rapid Application Development (RAD) model, incorporating feedback from CARE Center staff and a select group of students. The evaluation will be based on a pilot test assessing the system's usability and its perceived impact on daily operational efficiency within the Guihulngan campus.
Limitations: The study acknowledges several limitations. The prototype is designed for the specific context of the Guihulngan CARE Center; its applicability to larger campuses or different administrative units may be limited. Due to thesis-level resource constraints, the system will implement basic security measures and will not include advanced features like psychological test automation, detailed clinical note-taking modules, or full mobile application development. The evaluation's findings, derived from a small user sample over a short period, will indicate preliminary trends rather than provide statistically generalizable results on long-term effectiveness. The system's functionality also assumes basic internet connectivity and user digital literacy and does not automate the core, professional delivery of counseling or empowerment services themselves. These delimitations ensure the project remains focused, feasible, and directly addresses the identified inefficiencies in administrative record-keeping.
Research Methodology
This study employs a Descriptive-Developmental Research design to systematically investigate and address the inefficiencies of the current manual processes at the CARE Center of Negros Oriental State University – Guihulngan City Campus (NORSU-G). The descriptive component systematically collects and analyzes detailed data on existing CARE Center operations, including core services such as counseling coordination (with departmental approvals/referrals), assessment activities (NAT handling, student profiles, needs assessment surveys), resource provision (support forms for working students, indigenous peoples, orphans, scholarships), and enhancement programs (activity implementation, attendance tracking, feedback/evaluation). This phase identifies strengths, weaknesses, bottlenecks, and user perceptions (from staff and students) related to paper-based tools like physical logbooks, manual filing, clearances, and fragmented documentation—highlighting issues such as time delays, data inaccuracies, record loss risks, and administrative workload.
The developmental component builds directly on these findings by designing, developing, and prototyping a web-based CARE Center Management System tailored to resolve identified problems. The system automates and centralizes key workflows—such as digital counseling requests/approvals, NAT application/scheduling/results management, online resource/support forms, survey aggregation for event planning (orientations/symposia), attendance/proof tracking, scholarship processing, and feedback collection—aiming to enhance efficiency, reduce errors, improve data security/accessibility, and allow staff to prioritize direct student support. Development follows an iterative, user-centered process involving requirements gathering (via interviews, observations, and surveys), system design (e.g., use-case diagrams, database schema), prototyping/coding, testing (functionality, usability, security), and initial evaluation for refinement, ensuring the system is practical, scalable, and aligned with the resource-constrained environment of a Philippine SUC satellite campus.

CHAPTER  3
TECHNICAL BACKGROUND
This chapter presents the system analysis and design used in developing the NORSU-G Student Support and CARE Center Management System. It focuses on understanding and clearly defining what the system should do. The researchers followed the Rapid Application Development (RAD) model, which uses an iterative process. This approach emphasizes quick prototyping and continuous user feedback instead of long planning stages.

System Analysis and Design

Educational institutions and their support offices use different types of information systems for their daily operations. The development of the NORSU-G Student Support and CARE Center Management System followed the RAD approach. Unlike traditional linear models, the RAD phases are shorter and iterative. This helps ensure the final system truly meets the actual needs of the users.
The RAD model consists of the following phases:
Requirements Planning. In this initial phase, the researchers conducted a preliminary investigation to identify the main problems in the CARE Center. They observed inefficiencies in physical logbooks, manual counseling referrals, and paper-based tracking. The team defined the project scope and determined the basic digital requirements needed by the CARE Center. This phase was kept short to allow quick movement to the design stage.
User Design (Iterative Prototyping). This is the main phase of the RAD model. The researchers worked closely with the users. Based on the initial requirements, they created rapid prototypes of the database and user interfaces. Students and staff gave feedback on the layout and flow of the admissions, counseling, and scholarship modules. The researchers then revised the prototypes based on this feedback in real time.
Construction. In this phase, the researchers focused on actual coding and development using the refined prototypes. Because the design had already been validated by users, the construction phase moved faster. The team built the features of the CARE Center Management System step by step, tested them, and integrated the components until the system became fully functional.
Cutover (Implementation and Security). This final phase involved deploying the system in the Guihulngan campus. The researchers completed all security features, including multi-level user authentication and role-based access control, to protect data and ensure system reliability. They also conducted user training and migrated data from the old manual system to the new digital system. This ensured the system is scalable, easy to maintain, and ready for long-term use.

System Architecture
The researchers utilized an Entity Relationship Diagram (ERD) as a key architectural tool to elaborate and define the data structure and specific views of the CARE Center Management System. An ERD is a graphical representation that depicts relationships among people, objects, concepts, and events within a database system. It serves to outline exactly what data is stored, such as counseling requests, support program endorsements, and event attendance, and how these records interact to keep the platform running effectively.



Figure 3. Entity Relationship Diagram of the CARE Center Management System
Figure 3 serves as a visual tool that shows the relationships among various entities in the system's database. This diagram is commonly used in system development to visualize, organize, and normalize complex data structures.
In the CARE Center web application, the ERD clearly illustrates the connections among the system's main components. For example, it shows a one-to-zero-or-one relationship between the applications table and the students table. This means an initial service application is used to create an official student record. The original application entry is then removed to avoid data redundancy. These relationships are shown through line connectors that link records from one table to another.
The ERD provides a clear visual representation of how different administrative functions are related. It helps in understanding the underlying data structure of the system and supports the overall design and development process.
By using the ERD, the researchers were able to plan and organize the connections among the functional components of the student support platform. This results in a well-structured and efficient database design that can securely handle the center’s operations and avoid the data problems of the previous manual filing system.
User Application
The web-based application provides features tailored for users of the NORSU-Guihulngan CARE Center. After logging into the portal, users can access different modules based on their roles.
Applicants can use the system for admission and NAT processing. Enrolled students can manage scholarships, events, forms, and feedback, and request counseling and support services. Through this portal, users can easily submit requests and track their progress.
The platform also offers a secure and centralized place for users to manage their digital identities. It includes functions for account activation, authentication, student profile management, and security settings. These features replace the uncertainty of manual processing and paper-based profiling, giving students a more accessible and secure support experience.
Overall, the digital system provides a simple and user-friendly way for applicants and students to access guidance services. By automatically organizing processes and tracking all transactions, the system promotes transparency and supports continuous improvement in campus services.

CHAPTER 4
        SUMMARY OF FINDINGS, CONCLUSION, AND RECOMMENDATIONS                                                                                                                                                                                                                                
Summary
The purpose of this study is to develop a web-based Student Support and CARE Center Management System for Negros Oriental State University (NORSU) – Guihulngan City Campus. The system aims to replace manual, paper-based processes with a centralized digital platform. The current manual procedures in the CARE Center were evaluated to identify operational bottlenecks and determine the functional requirements needed to address them.
Current Manual Procedures
CARE Staff: When asked if manual tasks were manageable, they agreed (Overall Mean: 3.73). They have adapted well to verifying student data (Mean: 4.43, Rank 1), organizing physical requirements (Mean: 4.14, Rank 2), and retrieving files (Mean: 4.00, Rank 3), despite physical limitations.
Faculty: When asked if current coordination was effective, they agreed (Overall Mean: 4.07). They found monitoring student participation (Mean: 4.24, Rank 1), referral procedures (Mean: 4.20, Rank 2), and applicant screening (Mean: 4.18, Rank 3) to be functioning adequately.
Students: When asked if manual procedures were easy and systematic, they disagreed (Overall Mean: 2.54). They rejected the positive statements in all areas, indicating that scheduling profiling, submitting support applications, and registering for seminars are difficult and slow.

Problems Encountered Due to Current Manual Procedures
CARE Staff: Despite managing baseline tasks, they strongly agreed that they face severe burdens: locating past service records during peak periods causes massive delays (Mean: 4.71, Rank 1), physical records are prone to damage (Mean: 4.57, Rank 2), and consolidating manual reports is tedious (Mean: 4.57, Rank 3). Overall Mean: 4.46 (Strongly Agree).
Students: Disorganized manual attendance signing during events (Mean: 3.80, Rank 1), severe data privacy risks (Mean: 3.67, Rank 2), and logbook congestion (Mean: 3.66, Rank 3). Overall Mean: 3.67 (Agree).
Faculty: Manual documentation for applicant screening increases workload (Mean: 3.76, Rank 1), and tracking endorsed support requests is time-consuming (Mean: 3.66, Rank 2). Overall Mean: 3.46 (Agree).
Required Functional Components of the Proposed System
CARE Staff: Secure and instant retrieval of student records (Mean: 4.71, Rank 1), reduction in manual data entry (Mean: 4.71, Rank 2), and automated report generation (Mean: 4.57, Rank 3). Overall Mean: 4.58 (Strongly Agree).
Faculty: Processing and monitoring of student support endorsements (Mean: 4.54, Rank 1), streamlining counseling referrals (Mean: 4.48, Rank 2), and faster coordination (Mean: 4.36, Rank 3). Overall Mean: 4.28 (Strongly Agree).
Students: Satisfaction with the digital transition (Mean: 4.50, Rank 1), ability to check application status anytime (Mean: 4.45, Rank 2), and convenient access outside campus (Mean: 4.40, Rank 3). Overall Mean: 4.40 (Strongly Agree).
The study’s findings are based on data collected from 700 respondents (643 students, 50 faculty members, and 7 CARE staff) at NORSU-Guihulngan City Campus. The evaluation showed a clear link between users’ daily administrative tasks, their frustrations, and the features they requested. For CARE staff, although they manage to keep the manual system running, reliance on physical files causes long delays in finding records and makes report consolidation very tedious. This creates a strong need for instant digital retrieval and automated reporting. For faculty members, manually screening applicants and tracking support requests adds a heavy burden to their daily workload. They urgently need a system that can automatically track student endorsements. For students, using paper or third-party forms for profiling creates privacy risks. Manual logbooks cause office congestion. Attendance lists for events are often lost due to overcrowding when students sign in. These problems greatly increase their need for a secure and accessible online portal.

Findings
The majority of the findings in this study came from the analysis of data collected from 700 purposively selected respondents who are primary users of the CARE Center. The data clearly showed that the current manual procedures are the main cause of operational problems in the campus. While staff and faculty agreed they could manage the manual system, they strongly agreed that it creates heavy administrative burdens. In contrast, students clearly disagreed that the manual procedures were easy. However, when presented with the proposed web-based system designed to solve these specific problems, the overwhelming majority expressed strong support for the digital transition.
The developed CARE Center Management System aims to address the exact problems identified by the respondents by centralizing NAT applications, digitizing event attendance, securing student profiles in one place, and modernizing the delivery of student support services.

Recommendations
Based on the findings and conclusions of the study, the researchers offer the following recommendations:
System Expansion and Multi-Campus Integration Future developers should expand the system to other NORSU campuses. This requires redesigning the database to support multiple campuses, creating campus-specific modules, and ensuring smooth data sharing across the university.
Integration of Advanced AI-Driven Analytics Future versions of the system should include machine learning features for predictive analytics, such as identifying students who may need early support and analyzing student feedback. These tools will help the CARE Center provide better and more proactive student services.
Implementation of Secure Online Testing and Digital Signature Systems To remove the need for physical presence in document signing, future versions should add secure digital signatures and an online proctoring module for the National Admission Test (NAT). The system must comply with the Data Privacy Act (RA 10173), use strong encryption for sensitive records, and include proper identity verification.
Cross-Platform Development for Mobile Devices The current system is web-based only. Future development should create mobile applications for both Android and iOS using frameworks like React Native or Flutter. This will allow students to receive push notifications for counseling appointments, scholarship deadlines, and other important updates.
Infrastructure Upgrades for University-Wide Deployment If the system is adopted across the university for a large number of students, developers should improve the database performance, use stronger cloud servers with load balancing, and strengthen cybersecurity measures including encryption and automatic backups.
Implementation of Biometric Authentication and Advanced Role-Based Access Future versions should include biometric login options such as fingerprint or facial recognition. User roles should also be refined with detailed permission settings. This will enhance security for sensitive counseling records and improve administrative efficiency.
Adoption of Offline-First Synchronization Capabilities To support areas with unstable internet connection, the system should be developed as a Progressive Web Application (PWA). This will allow students to fill out forms or check schedules offline, with data automatically syncing when internet is available.
Longitudinal Evaluation and Continuous System Refinement Researchers and developers should regularly collect feedback from students and faculty, conduct system performance reviews, and make continuous improvements based on usage data. This will ensure the system stays relevant to student needs and adapts to new technologies and regulations.




Conclusion
This study developed a digital management system to improve student support services and administrative processes for CARE Center staff and faculty members at NORSU-Guihulngan City Campus. Once NAT applications, post-NAT admission screenings, student profile activation, counseling and support requests, event attendance, logbooks, and scholarship applications are processed through the system, all information is automatically recorded and validated. This ensures accuracy and makes student records easier to track. As a result, counselors and staff can perform their duties smoothly, while students and incoming students receive faster and better-organized services. The researchers believe that implementing this system will greatly benefit students, faculty, and CARE staff by simplifying record management, student support, needs assessment, and other related services. The CARE Center Management System will improve the accuracy, security, and accessibility of student data. It will also reduce reliance on manual processes, leading to more efficient operations and a more modern and organized university support system.
